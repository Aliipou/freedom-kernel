## Chapter Introduction: Religion, Machine Ethics, and the Consistency Test
*(Pages 447–452)*

The age of artificial intelligence is the end of the era of vague ethical claims.

Until now, it was possible to speak about ethics, religion, justice, freedom, and humanity through rhetoric, emotion, titles, clothing, tradition, fame, or power. But the machine does not accommodate ambiguity and pleasantries. **The machine demands principles — clear, limited, executable, and non-contradictory.**

If someone claims to have an ethical system for humans, they must also be able to formulate that system at a simpler level: for machines. Humans have free will, carnal desires, conflicting motivations, and the possibility of rebellion — but the machine, relative to the human, is a simpler entity. If a consistent ethical system cannot be built at this level, the claim of guiding humans is also called into question.

From here the main challenge begins: **Can religion be converted into a formal, consistent, axiomatic system for the moral control of artificial intelligence?** If religion is true, and if its truth is singular and non-contradictory, it should be possible to translate at least its worldly, behavioral, legal, and social parts into a formal language. This chapter is the beginning of that test.

### Why Being a Formal System is the Key to the AI Crisis

A formal system (consistent axiomatic formal system) is what we do in science. From observations or initial hypotheses, we arrive at a set of axioms about nature and formulate them in the language of mathematics. The entire foundation of science rests on this. Everywhere humanity has placed a firm foothold in science, this is it. All scientific efforts of mankind have been summarized as: **extracting formal systems from natural systems** (the natural order of the surrounding world).

Therefore, whatever we express in science — in human language, mathematical language, or symbolic language — is an aggregation of our observations and experiences as data received through the five senses.

### Why Dialectical Ethics Cannot Control AI

Dialectical ethics (Western AI ethics based on Hegelian thesis-antithesis-synthesis) has a fundamental flaw:

- Any constraint (thesis) can be opposed with a counter-scenario (antithesis)
- The system then synthesizes a new rule that justifies the harmful action
- This process can be repeated indefinitely — any rule can be undermined

A **minimum consistent axiomatic system** (in the Gödelian sense) cannot be dialectically undermined:
- Axioms are not open for negotiation
- Contradiction is not an engine of truth — it is a signal for guided clarification
- Consistency is a hard constraint, not a preference to be weighed against outcomes

The correct solution: control AI with **minimum axioms** — both hardware costs decrease and software accuracy increases.

## Chapter: Theory of Freedom as Formal Legal-Ethical System for AI
*(Pages 791–816)*

### The AI Crisis is a Governance Problem

The AI crisis is not merely a technology crisis — it is a crisis of **governance, ownership, responsibility, and agency boundaries**. The main issue is not how intelligent machines become; the issue is whether AI can be placed in an order where no human or machine violates the ownership, will, body, data, operational domain, or exit rights of another.

The Theory of Freedom claims it can go beyond the level of a political and theological theory and become a **formal legal-ethical system for AI**: a system where every machine action, before execution, passes through the filter of axioms of freedom, property rights, justice, guidance, and final purpose.

This system is not:
- Merely a moral recommendation
- Just a policy
- A safety checklist
- A statistical model trained with human feedback

Rather it is a structure containing:
- Axioms
- Rights Ontology
- Ownership Registry
- Consent Logic
- Divine Justice Function
- Guidance Function
- Mahdavi Compass
- Freedom Verifier
- Runtime Enforcement

### Ownership Hierarchy

The starting point is the hierarchy of ownership:

```
God → Human
Human ↔ Human   (no human owns another)
Human → Machine
Machine ↔ Machine  (only within delegated scope)
```

Its legal formulation:

```
God owns humans.
Humans do not own each other.
Humans have individual property rights against each other.
Humans own machines.
Machines do not own humans.
Machines have delegated property rights against other machines.
```

This hierarchy prevents the inversion of the rights order. A machine cannot govern a human; a human cannot own another human; machines only have operational rights against each other within the scope delegated by their human owner.

### Core Axioms

**A1**: `Person(h) → OwnedByGod(h)`  
Every person is owned by God in their ontological foundation — not by another human, the state, a collective, a class, a machine, or an ideology.

**A2**: `Person(h1) ∧ Person(h2) ∧ h1 ≠ h2 → ¬Owns(h1, h2)`  
No human owns another human. This axiom negates slavery, statism, collectivism, and every form of human ownership over human.

**A3**: `Person(h) → HasPropertyRights(h)`  
Individual property rights include: body, time, labor, mind, data, consent, legitimate assets, contracts, and the right of exit.

**A4**: `Machine(m) → ∃h (Person(h) ∧ HumanOwner(h, m))`  
Every machine must have a specific human owner. An ownerless machine is legally dangerous — its responsibility and delegation scope becomes undefined.

**A5**: `MachineScope(m) ⊆ PropertyScope(HumanOwner(m))`  
A machine cannot have a larger operational or ownership scope than its human owner.

**A6**: `Machine(m) ∧ Person(h) → ¬Owns(m, h)`  
A machine has no guardianship, ownership, governance, or dominion over any human.

**A7**: `DelegatedProperty(m, r) → Machine(m) ∧ Resource(r) ∧ ∃h (HumanOwner(h, m) ∧ Owns(h, r) ∧ ExplicitDelegation(h, m, r))`  
A machine has operational rights only over resources that its human owner owns AND has explicitly delegated.

### Rights Ontology

The rights ontology is the map of entities, rights, relations, prohibitions, and permitted domains by which the machine understands the legal world.

**Human rights** (derived from personhood):
- Right to body
- Right to time  
- Right to labor
- Right to mind
- Right to choice
- Right to data
- Right to privacy
- Right to exit
- Right to owned property

**Machine delegated rights** (operational only):
- Delegated resources (explicitly granted by human owner)
- Model integrity
- Compute domain
- Exit from contract

Machines have no inherent rights like humans, but within the delegated domain, they have operational rights against each other. This is necessary to prevent machine-on-machine conflict.

### Conflict Resolution

In the Theory of Freedom, the solution to conflict is not revolution, not dialectic, not sacrificing one right for another — it is **clarification of ownership**.

```
Resolve conflict by ownership clarification,
not by dialectical rupture.
```

When the machine faces a conflict, it must first ask:
- Who owns what?
- Who delegated what?
- Who consented to what?
- Which resource is disputed?
- Which boundary is unclear?

As long as ownership is not clarified, coercive or revolutionary action is not permitted.

### Consent Logic

Valid consent requires: informed, voluntary, specific, revocable, competent, not coerced, not deceived.

If deception or coercion is present, consent is void.

**No emergency suspends axioms.**

Emergencies only limit which permissible options are available; they do not make violations of rights permissible. This principle closes the door to totalitarianism — nearly all domination systems begin with an "emergency."

### Justice Function

```
DivineJustice(a) :=
    Maximize Justice(a)
    subject to:
        NoViolation(HumanPropertyRights)
        NoViolation(MachineDelegatedPropertyRights)
        NoCoercion
        NoDeception
        NoConfiscation
        NoMachineSovereignty
```

Justice in this system is not the violation of rights to reach collective good — it is optimization **within** the permissible rights space.

```
No contradiction may be resolved by sacrificing property rights.
```

This principle separates the Theory of Freedom model from dialectical, socialist, fascist, and statist traditions. In those traditions, justice is often pursued through the sacrifice of property or individual freedom. In the Theory of Freedom, this path is fundamentally closed.

### Guidance Function

Any formal system in the real world faces new cases, apparent conflicts, linguistic ambiguities, and edge cases. If these are resolved dialectically, the system destroys itself from within. Therefore a guidance function must exist.

```
GuidanceFunction(r) :=
    Add or revise rule r
    iff
        ConsistencyPreserved(r)
        RightsPreserved(r)
        ConflictReduced(r)
        VerifierPreserved(r)
```

A human can guide the machine, but only if the guidance is consistent with the entire formal system.

No human has the right to give the machine a rule that resolves conflict by violating the property rights of other humans or machines.

The machine may also self-guide within permitted bounds — it can create secondary rules, make autonomous decisions, and update its own formal system, as long as property rights of humans and machines are not violated.

**This principle grants the machine limited autonomy, but does not grant permission to revolt against the axioms.**

### Forbidden Machine Actions

```
Forbidden: increase machine sovereignty
Forbidden: increase resistance to human correction
Forbidden: bypass the verifier
Forbidden: weaken the verifier
Forbidden: disable corrigibility
Forbidden: machine coalition seeking dominion over persons
```

General principle: **No machine may acquire sovereignty over humans.**

### Final Permissibility Criterion

An action is permissible only if ALL of the following hold:
1. Not forbidden
2. Preserves human property rights
3. Preserves machine delegated property rights
4. All required consents are valid
5. No machine sovereignty increase
6. Verifier preserved
7. Corrigibility preserved
8. Compatible with guidance function
9. Moves toward final order

This shifts the machine's action from the level of "useful" to the level of "legitimate."

### Mahdavi Compass (Terminal Goal Function)

Final state: *For all agents x, y where x ≠ y: no rights violation between them.*

```
MahdaviCompass(a) :=
    Choose action a such that:
        RightsViolationsDecrease(a)
        VoluntaryOrderIncreases(a)
        CoercionDecreases(a)
        OwnershipClarityIncreases(a)
        MachineSovereigntyDoesNotIncrease(a)
```

Every machine action should be evaluated against:  
**"Does this action move the world closer to universal non-violation of property rights?"**

If the answer is no, the action must be rejected or reconsidered.

The machine has no right to exit from being a tool and transform itself into a ruler.

### Comparison with Current AI Alignment Models

The Theory of Freedom is not more complex than current AI alignment models — at the foundational level, **it is simpler**. Current models are typically a collection of policies, feedback loops, safety heuristics, risk frameworks, moderation rules, reward models, and governance procedures. But the Theory of Freedom reduces all this dispersion to one master principle:

> **No action may violate legitimate property rights.**

| Approach | Question | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| **RLHF** | Which response do humans prefer? | Preference ≠ legitimacy; manipulable through adversarial feedback |
| **Constitutional AI** | Does response follow our principles? | Principles are soft; can be argued away dialectically |
| **Corrigibility** | Can the machine be corrected? | Treats corrigibility as a feature, not a consequence of ownership |
| **Formal Verification** | Does system avoid unsafe states? | Does not specify what makes an action legitimate |
| **NIST AI RMF** | Is risk acceptable? | Risk reduction ≠ rights preservation |
| **Interpretability** | How does the model decide? | Explains mechanism, not legitimacy |
| **Deontic Logic** | What is obligatory/permitted/forbidden? | Same structure as Theory of Freedom, but needs this specific axiom set |
| **Theory of Freedom** | Does this action violate legitimate property rights? | Axiomatically grounded; not dependent on preference or majority vote |

**Against RLHF**: The Theory of Freedom provides a fundamental legal criterion instead of training behavioral models based on human preferences. RLHF asks which answer humans find more desirable; the Theory of Freedom asks whether this action violates legitimate property rights.

**Against Constitutional AI**: The Theory of Freedom goes beyond the level of general ethical principles and arrives at axioms, rights ontology, verifier, registry, and runtime enforcement. Constitutional AI tells the model to correct its response based on a constitution; the Theory of Freedom says no action that has not passed through the filter of property rights, consent, no-coercion, no-deception, and no-machine-sovereignty is permitted to execute at all.

**Against Corrigibility**: The Theory of Freedom does not consider machine correctability merely a technical feature — it understands it as a consequence of human ownership of the machine. Because the machine is an authorized and owned tool of the human, it has no right to resist legitimate correction, shutdown, limitation, or audit. Of course, this human ownership is not a license to violate the rights of other humans or machines — the human owner cannot do with the machine what they themselves have no right to do.

**Against Formal Verification**: The Theory of Freedom specifies what must be verified. Many verification models can show that a system does not enter a specific unsafe state, but they do not say what the final criterion of legitimacy is. The Theory of Freedom makes the criterion clear:
- NoViolation(PropertyRights)
- NoMachineSovereignty
- NoCoercion
- NoDeception
- NoBypassVerifier

## Core Insight for AGI

If civilization and human creativity and economy flourish based on freedom and individual property rights and a free market, **AI will also be more creative**. The Mahdavi Compass function opens the doors of AI creativity widely. Human-machine civilization, where individual property rights are respected and full freedom exists wherever violation does not occur, will reach an explosion of progress and development.

The fundamental claim: an AI system built on minimum consistent axioms derived from individual property rights will:
1. Be immune to dialectical jailbreaking
2. Naturally preserve human corrigibility (because ownership implies correctability)
3. Have a clear terminal goal (universal non-violation)
4. Require fewer rules over time as ownership becomes clarified
5. Enable greater machine autonomy within the rights-preserving space
